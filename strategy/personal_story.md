# Personal Story — Why This Matters

*Why are you building this? Who are you trying to help? What moment made this feel urgent?*

---

## The Moment

I was at brunch with my mom on a Sunday morning. It was supposed to be relaxing — just the two of us catching up. Then she remembered she hadn't set her lineups or placed her bets. What had been a calm morning turned stressful in seconds.

She started asking me rapid-fire questions: Which teams should she pick? What about the over/under? Should she take the spread? I gave her the best answers I could — one team has a stronger offensive line, another is on a hot streak — but honestly, I wasn't confident. I felt guilty. If she lost money because of my advice, that was on me.

We tried opening ChatGPT together to get some analysis. It was decent but generic, and not something she'd remember to do on her own week after week. I showed her ESPN and a few stats sites, but they were walls of data and long-form articles — not what someone needs when they're trying to make a decision in the next 20 minutes. The sportsbook apps were worse — screens full of parlays and betting lines that read like a foreign language if you don't already know the terminology.

She lost money that week. And it wasn't a one-time thing. During football season, she reaches out several times a week asking for help with her bets. The whole experience — the scrambling, the anxiety, the emotional decision-making — repeats on a loop.

That brunch was the moment I thought: *there has to be something better than this.*

## Who You're Protecting

My mom — called "Susan" in this project. She's not a degenerate gambler. She's a woman who enjoys football, wants to participate in the culture around betting, and loses $20 here, $20 there because she doesn't have the context to make informed decisions. Over the course of a season, those small losses add up. As she gets older, I want her spending her money wisely — not bleeding it out on emotional bets.

But it's not just her. I imagine there are sons and daughters all over the country who get the same anxious texts on Sunday mornings. And beyond family, this extends to anyone who can't read a sportsbook fluently — people of all ages who are drawn into betting by how easy the apps make it to *place* a bet, without any help understanding *whether* they should.

## Why You (a Product Designer) Are the Right Person to Solve This

The data exists. The analysis exists. The problem isn't a lack of information — it's that no one has designed the experience of *understanding* it.

Sportsbooks are designed to get you to bet. Stats sites are designed for analysts. Content sites are designed for engaged fans who already speak the language. Nobody has designed for Susan — the person who wants to make a smart decision but doesn't have 30 minutes or a statistics degree to get there.

That's a design problem. Not a data problem. Not an engineering problem. The complexity needs to be absorbed by the product so the user doesn't have to carry it. That's what product designers do — and that's why I'm the right person to build this.
