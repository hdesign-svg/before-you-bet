# Market Landscape — What Exists, Where the Gap Is

---

## Existing Products

### Sportsbooks (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM)
- **What they do:** Let you place bets. Surfaces lines, odds, parlays, and promotions. Some offer light editorial content and "picks."
- **Who they serve:** Active bettors who already understand how betting works. The UI assumes fluency.
- **Gap:** Designed to maximize betting volume, not understanding. A beginner can place a bet in seconds but has no way to understand *why* a line is set where it is. The experience actively discourages pausing to think.

### Stats/Data Sites (ESPN, PFF, Football Outsiders)
- **What they do:** Surface game previews, stats, power rankings, depth charts, injury reports, expert analysis articles.
- **Who they serve:** Engaged football fans and fantasy players who already read stats fluently.
- **Gap:** Information-dense, not insight-dense. A beginner drowns in acronyms (DVOA, EPA, ATS) and long-form content. There's no "so what?" layer — you're expected to draw your own conclusions.

### Betting Content (Action Network, Covers, VSiN)
- **What they do:** Provide expert picks, betting trends, odds comparisons, and editorial analysis specifically for bettors.
- **Who they serve:** Intermediate-to-advanced bettors who understand spreads, totals, and market movement.
- **Gap:** Content assumes the reader already knows betting terminology. Articles are long and analytical. The value proposition is "better picks" — not "better understanding." A beginner doesn't know enough to evaluate whether a pick is good.

### AI/Prediction Tools (ChatGPT, Perplexity, various sports AI apps)
- **What they do:** Generate on-demand analysis when you ask the right questions.
- **Who they serve:** Anyone, in theory — but realistically, people who know what questions to ask.
- **Gap:** Requires initiative and prompt literacy. The output is generic unless you're specific. It's a tool, not a product — there's no curation, no editorial voice, no consistent experience. Most importantly, it's not a habit. People don't remember to open ChatGPT every Sunday.

---

## The White Space

Every existing product assumes the user either:
1. Already understands betting (sportsbooks, betting content)
2. Already reads stats fluently (ESPN, PFF)
3. Knows what questions to ask (AI tools)

**Nobody is designing for the person who doesn't know what they don't know.**

The gap isn't data. It's translation. It's taking the complexity that already exists and presenting it in a way that a beginner can understand, trust, and act on — without studying, scrolling, or asking for help.

Before You Bet sits in the space between "I want to bet" and "I placed a bet" — the decision moment where clarity matters most and nothing currently exists.

---

## Positioning Statement

**Before You Bet** is a clarity layer for beginner bettors that translates complex sports data into plain-English understanding before they place a bet — unlike sportsbooks which optimize for action, stats sites which assume expertise, and AI tools which require you to know what to ask.
